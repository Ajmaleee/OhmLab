Resistor History, How They Work & Ohm's Law

Resistors began as simple wire coils and carbon compositions used to tame current in early radio and telegraph equipment. Today they come as precise metal-film parts or tiny surface mount chips that fit on a fingernail. A resistor works by converting electrical energy into heat, limiting current and creating predictable voltage drops. Ohm's Law explains this relationship: V = I Ã— R, linking voltage, current, and resistance in every circuit. Fun fact: the first widely adopted resistor color code standard was introduced in the 1920s to help technicians read values quickly without printing tiny numbers. That same color band system still appears on many through-hole resistors today.
